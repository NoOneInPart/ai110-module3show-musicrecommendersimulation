## 3. How the Model Works  

Explain your scoring approach in simple language.  

Prompts:  

- What features of each song are used (genre, energy, mood, etc.)  
- What user preferences are considered  
- How does the model turn those into a score  
- What changes did you make from the starter logic  

Avoid code here. Pretend you are explaining the idea to a friend who does not program.

> The genre, mood, energy, and acoustic metadata are used when recommending songs. Up to 5 score points are given to a song based on how well it matches a user's preferred energy level, 1 score point is given if the song matches a user's preferred mood and genre respectively, and up to 0.5 points are awarded depending on how acoustic a song is and whether a user likes acoustic songs. Originally, the acoustic score was a fixed bonus depending on whether a song passed a threshold of 0.5 acousticness, but now it scales depending on how acoustic the song is.

## 5. Strengths  

Where does your system seem to work well  

Prompts:  

- User types for which it gives reasonable results  
- Any patterns you think your scoring captures correctly

> This model works best for users who like their songs to all be roughly the same energy level. It generally collects the genres and moods of songs that a user likes without relying too heavily on it. For instance, a user that likes high energy pop will get high energy songs, pop ones at the top.

## 6. Limitations and Bias 

Where the system struggles or behaves unfairly. 

Prompts:  

- Features it does not consider  
- Genres or moods that are underrepresented  
- Cases where the system overfits to one preference  
- Ways the scoring might unintentionally favor some users  

> This recommendation model does not implement tempo_bpm, valence, or danceablility as they aren't currently part of the user profile. Genres and moods use exact-string matching so nearby genres and moods, like pop -> indie pop, are still considered completely separate. With the current song catalog, there are no tracks with an energy level between 0.46 and 0.68, so users preferring a mid-energy level would not be served tracks that match their preferences as well compared to users who prefer a high or low energy level. There are also more lofi and pop tracks in the catalog compared to other genres.

## 7. Evaluation  

How you checked whether the recommender behaved as expected. 

Prompts:  

- Which user profiles you tested  
- What you looked for in the recommendations  
- What surprised you  
- Any simple tests or comparisons you ran  

No need for numeric metrics unless you created some.

> I tested "high energy pop," "chill lofi," "deep intense rock," "acoustic headbanger," and "happy classical" profiles. I looked at the results to see if they sorta made sense for the user profiles, which they mostly did outside the latter two edge case profiles. The pop profile surfaced high energy songs, which caught all the pop songs. The lofi profile surfaced low energy songs which caught all the lofi songs. The rock profile surfaced high energy songs, alongside the genre scoring bonus it caught the one rock song and threw in an industrial metal song. The acoustic headbanger profile relied mostly on energy because a high energy acoustic song is kinda contradictory and the acoustic score bonus was tuned pretty low. The happy classical profile successfully returned the one classical song and some of the lofi tracks, which I think makes sense. I was surprised at how well this simple model works despite it relying pretty heavily on energy. I tested boosting the energy weight and halving the genre weight as the assignment suggested, which made the model perform worse as it started to perform worse at recommending people the genre they like.

## 9. Personal Reflection  

A few sentences about your experience.  

Prompts:  

- What you learned about recommender systems  
- Something unexpected or interesting you discovered  
- How this changed the way you think about music recommendation apps  

> I learned that recommendation systems are very fickle and flatten everything about a song to metrics like energy and "danceability," which eliminates a lot of nuance about what makes a song good, yet it is interesting that it still kinda works. This sort of rule-based recommendation system is very cheap to run and transparent, but is too simple; it takes genre and mood names literally and any variation is treated as something completely different, and it relies too much on recommending songs of a similar energy level. I can see the appeal for training machine learning models to do recommendations, though the algorithm really does turn into a black box at that point. I have a bit more respect for music recommendation algorithms in apps like Spotify now, it's just trying to do its best with what it has.🥲

> I used Claude to generate the recommendation code and suggest how to build the logic. It suggested code that worked very well, though I believe it suggested a fixed score bonus for acoustic songs before I eventually steered it into making the acoustic score bonus scale according to how acoustic the song was. I glanced through all the suggestions to make sure it wasn't doing more than it needed to do. If I were to extend this project, I would implement a more extensive user profile so the recommender can use more song features when making recommendations (see #8).
